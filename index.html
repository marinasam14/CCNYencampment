City College and Columbia University Were Raided the Same Night. Why Did We Only Hear About Columbia?

While the Ivy League campus drew media attention, the NYPD carried out brutal mass arrests at the public university just 20 blocks north.

By Marina Samuel

November 30, 2024



From the evening of April 30 to the early hours of May 1, the CUNY Gaza Solidarity Encampment reached a dramatic conclusion when the university administration authorized the NYPD to enter the City College of New York campus and sweep the encampment, arresting nearly 200 people, hours after they raided Columbia University in the same militaristic fashion.



In the midst of the chaos, reporters raced to break the news of the concurrent raids on the two Harlem campuses, with Columbia University’s receiving a considerable amount of media attention compared to City College’s, although the latter resulted in more arrests.

The public institution with a 75% student of color population, saw 173 people arrested between the 30th and the 1st. As the trial is set to begin next year for 8 demonstrators who rejected plea deals, in favor of having their time in court.



When reviewing the coverage conducted by some of the most popular news publications in the United States, those sites were about 7 times more likely to write about Columbia than City College. The disparities in initial coverage continued to conceal the protestors unequal treatment from the media and law enforcement alike.





Manhattan DA Alvin Bragg dropped the charges against 31 of the 42 arrested in Hamilton Hall yet only 9 of the 22 arrested in the Wille Administration building had theirs dismissed. Those arrested in the Wille Administration building faced felony charges while those who occupied Hamilton Hall were charged with misdemeanors. Some City College protestors were also detained for over 48 hours without seeing a judge, above the legal limit. The focus on Columbia allowed for these inequities to go underreported by major media outlets.



“A lot of mainstream journalists were coming onto campus with a narrative to fulfill," said Leon Orlov-Sullivan, former Editor-in-Chief of CCNY’s The Paper who had also live streamed the night on Instagram.



"It was almost like they already had their articles written by the time that they got there.”
